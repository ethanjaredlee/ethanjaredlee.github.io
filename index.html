Takeaways

First and foremost in this class, I learned a lot about the interactions between Asian Americans and the structures in society and how Orientalism affects this.

Orientalism & Body Image

A lot of what we talked about in class came back to this idea of Orientalism and how it affects the daily lives of Asian Americans. Orientalism originated from this idea that there was a distinction between people from the East and people from the West and that Westerners viewed themselves as superior to the East.

This idea leaks into the physical perception of those from "the Orient" where Asians are usually seen as smaller and weaker than their Western counterparts. This is further emphasized by pop culture where it's very rare for there to be a physically imposing Asian lead. Take the popular Marvel Studios franchise for example. There's been ~20 movies and dozens of superheros and supervillians, without any Asian representation. Evan Gong from the Buzzfeed video We Trained Like Superheroes For 30 Days says it well (0:28): "We rarely get to see Asian characters that are powerful, reliable..." While there are figures like Bruce Lee and Jackie Chan who are action stars, they succeed in their roles more through finesse, speed, and martial arts skill rather than pure physical prowess.

Living in a society that is influenced by the mindset that Asians are inherently smaller/weaker coupled with the absense of a public figure that refutes this, is definitely a factor in what discourages people from starting to workout at gyms like SPAC along with all the other barriers to entry for weightlifting.



Asian America in SPAC

What SPAC represents for Asian Americans isn't just a normal gym. It's a gym that's included in tuition fees. It's the most convenient location for a gym for 90% of the students living on campus. But most importantly, it's the gym almost every student on campus who works out goes to. Almost everyone will know someone who goes there that is more than willing to teach someone how to lift. In my case, I had been lifting for years before coming to college and already planned on utilizing SPAC as a space to destress. In Justin's case, he had his friend George who was able to show him the ropes and physically support him by working out together. In Jane's case, she was able to get the support she needed to start by getting a workout program from her friend.

Asian America can be found in SPAC, less in the physical space of space, and more in the empowering capabilities that SPAC provides. SPAC allows for Asian Americans to change their physical appearance and become bigger, stronger, and more confident in themselves. As we learned through Justin and Jane, by changing how you physically look, you change how you think others view you which is the first step to defining a space in which Asian America exists in place of Orientalism. By allowing Asian Americans to first internally believe in ideas that oppose the Orientalist views, they start to defy the expectations and eventually the reality differs from the stereotype so much that the stereotype is invalid.

Looking Forward

Contrasting my experience with Justin's, I had an easy introduction to things like weightlifting and a different image of what an Asian male should look like: my dad. He started lifting from a fairly early age too and always had a focus on health and lifting. Most Asian kids growing up never had that, including Justin, and so SPAC serves as a great introduction.

Looking forward, there are more sources emerging that serve as a model of a non-Orientalist physical representation. I previously mentioned that there is no Asian representation in MCU films that have come out so far, but rumors have it that there's a new film Shang-Chi that's coming out with an Asian lead. In addition to that, there are other role models like Joseph Lee that serve to help change the stereotype of how an Asian should look.
